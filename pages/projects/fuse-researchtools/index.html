Tooling up your user research

During a standard discovery phase we tend to gather a lot of information, either from analytics or user interview. Therefore it is important to give a second look to this data, interpret it and make sense of them.

This is where some best practices and tools can help refining this info and come with clear interpretation in your discovery document.

Specifics

  • Employer: Fuse
  • Period: Q3 2021 to Q4 2021
  • My role: Design Manager

    Problem: Moving away from raw data and transcripts

    A list of numbers, several transcripts of user interviews that do not look enticing to read and a a list of reasons as to why "this feature" will solve users problems problem. This is likely the kind of discovery document many of us have had to get through in the past.

    Discovery documents should not only be a loose collection of data with assumptions but should also filter them and explain our reasoning. At Fuse our discovery documents tended to be just like this. UX designers were not involved during the discovery phase and Product managers were responsible of putting this document together with all the raw data and transcripts from interviews.

    Discovery documents should not only be a loose collection of data with assumptions.

    Context: Involving UX more during discovery

    As stated we had two issues, the lack of data intepretation and ownership from UX during the discovery phase. However the blame was not on the product managers because they had to carry all the responsibilities of this phase upon their shoulders. Leading them to be quite overwhelmed most of the time.

    Since we were also shaking up our design process We had a perfect opportunity to reconsider this critical phase of the design process and redefine the balance of ownership between the product manager and the product designer.

    Solution: Ownership and introducing tools

    Therefore we agreed user interviews and their interpretation should be led by designers. Of course the product manager would still be involved but we would take the responsibilities for this part.

    Aside from this I decided to introduce a set of practices and tools in order to help us interpreting the data we were receiving, especially coming from user interviews. These tools were made with Figma and Miro and could easily be reused across projects via a simple copy-paste.

    The angular stone of the research would sit in the empathy map that would lead to creating real life personas of our users. These personas would not only provide accurate info about our user needs (since they are based on real people) but could potentially be stored and reused for future similar projects.

    Finally I decided to make the most of Mixpanel's (our data analytics tool) features to set up a Dashboard to capture the relevant data and user journey funnels for helping with my design decisions.

    Outcomes: More impact from UX

    Down the line we have also solved another problem that I did not mention: The fact designers were not feeling involved and felt they had to design features based upon the product manager interpretations.

    From now on designers can have a critical impact on any project from the beginning and could also use a set of scalable and easy to use tools in Figma that anyone could contribute to.

    It was even more rewarding to see product managers realising we could actually draw out conclusions from research we could have never done without this type of approach and asking we keep doing so in the future.
